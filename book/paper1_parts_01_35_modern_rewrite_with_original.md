# On Faraday's Lines of Force: Modernized Rewrite With Original Text

# Part 01: Faraday's Lines of Force and the Need for Mathematical Form

The present state of electrical science does not permit a satisfactory theory unless *electrostatics*, *current electricity*, attraction, and induction are brought into one connected mathematical scheme. It is no longer enough to possess separate formulas for the distribution of electricity on conductors, separate laws for the action of currents, and separate rules for inductive effects. A useful theory must reproduce the established laws in all the cases already reduced to analysis, and must also furnish a method of calculation in limiting cases where the familiar formulas are no longer directly available.

The first necessity, therefore, is reduction. The science has accumulated a body of results whose retention in memory, merely as detached expressions, obstructs further progress. If I keep only the formulas, I may preserve correctness of calculation while losing sight of the physical relations that make the science intelligible. If, on the other hand, I begin with an overly literal physical hypothesis, I run the opposite risk: I may allow a convenient picture to harden into dogma, and by that means become blind to facts that do not fit the chosen mechanism.

I therefore require a mode of investigation in which a clear physical conception is always before the mind, while no stronger claim is made for that conception than the mathematics warrants. The borrowed picture must guide the understanding without tyrannizing over it. It must assist in tracing connexions among phenomena without obliging me to maintain that the borrowed mechanical image is the true and ultimate constitution of electricity.

The medium-like picture is thus retained deliberately. I do not introduce it as a final ontology, nor as a claim that electricity is literally a common fluid or that magnetic action is literally the motion of a visible substance. I retain it because distributed action in space is most readily understood when a continuous medium is imagined, and because the passage from one part of space to another is thereby made mathematically manageable as well as physically conceivable.

In this way I seek a middle path between bare calculation and premature mechanism. The equations are not sacrificed to imagery, nor is imagery banished in favor of abstraction alone. The picture remains subordinate, but indispensable.

To obtain physical ideas without prematurely adopting a physical theory, I must work through physical analogies. By such an analogy I mean not identity of mechanism, but agreement in mathematical form sufficient to let one class of phenomena illuminate another. Exact science aims at the determination of quantities by numerical relations; analogy gives those relations a sensible and manageable dress, so that the mind may move from one quantity to another without constantly losing sight of the thing signified.

The value of analogy lies precisely in its partial character. Two phenomena may differ widely in physical constitution and yet obey the same formal law. When this occurs, one may borrow the conceptual apparatus of the first to arrange the second, provided one remembers throughout that the agreement lies in the relations and not necessarily in the mechanism. The analogy is therefore a disciplined method of thought, not an invitation to careless identification.

The examples furnished by optics make this clear. The path of a ray through changing media and the path of a particle acted on by intense forces may agree in direction while disagreeing in everything else. The analogy may be useful, and even powerful, while still being incomplete. Likewise the analogy between light and the vibrations of an elastic medium may be fertile without being licensed to pronounce upon the ultimate essence of luminous action.

I adopt the same discipline here. A model may be physically suggestive, mathematically exact within its proper scope, and yet still remain merely an artificial method. Its office is to carry the reasoning and to connect the laws. It is not to overrule observation, nor to convert a resemblance of form into a claim of full identity. In the present inquiry, the medium is to be used for thought and calculation, not worshipped as an exhaustive account of nature.

The comparison between inverse-square attraction and steady heat conduction shows the kind of structural transfer I require. These subjects differ physically, yet their governing relations may be written in the same mathematical form. If I reinterpret the variables, one problem becomes another. In later language, one may say that the same *potential* structure is preserved while the physical meaning of the quantities is changed.

Thus *temperature*, *heat flux*, and *conductivity* may be set in formal correspondence with *potential*, directed action, and resistance. The important point is not that heat is electricity or that attraction is literally conduction, but that the mathematical dependencies have the same form. Once this is recognized, one gains a controlled method of transport from one subject to the other. The reasoning travels even where the physical vocabulary changes.

This is exactly the kind of transfer that the electrical theory requires. The laws of action at a distance have long possessed a settled analytical expression, yet they remain difficult to grasp physically when treated in naked algebra alone. The laws of a continuous medium, by contrast, bring before the mind contiguous action, passage through space, and distributed intensity. If both sets of laws share the same form, then the medium may serve as the intelligible representative of the action.

For that reason a continuous medium remains indispensable to my way of thinking. I do not introduce it merely as ornament. I require it because it supplies an intelligible picture of distributed action in space while the equations retain their full precision. The medium is not a decorative comparison added after the fact; it is the vehicle by which the mathematics is made physically apprehensible from the beginning.

My aim is therefore to place Faraday's mode of reasoning before the mathematical reader in a form that is both manageable and exact. I do not attempt to assert a finished mechanical theory of electricity, nor do I pretend to found the subject on experiments of my own. I seek instead to show that Faraday's ideas admit of precise mathematical development, and that their different classes of phenomena can be connected without abandoning the physical route by which those ideas were first obtained.

Faraday's language has often been regarded as powerful in suggestion but indefinite in calculation. I deny that this indefiniteness is essential. If one follows the line of thought strictly, keeping the geometrical and physical meaning of the quantities constantly before the mind, then the connexions among the phenomena may be made exact without depriving the theory of its fertility. The purpose is not to translate Faraday into a foreign system, but to develop what is already latent in his method.

Accordingly, I retain only those constructions that directly assist the understanding of electrical, magnetic, and galvanic phenomena. Where simple geometrical reasoning suffices, I use it. Where the subject grows more intricate, I use analytical notation. In either case, I keep the argument within the conceptual track laid down by Faraday.

The mathematical symbols are therefore not introduced as an alien superstructure, but as an instrument for displaying the consequences of ideas already present. What is borrowed from modern notation is only increased explicitness, not a change of intellectual allegiance.

I begin with lines of force. At every point in space there is a determinate direction in which a *positive test charge* would be urged, and the opposite direction for a negative one. The same holds for a north or south magnetic pole. If a curve is drawn so that its tangent at each point agrees with this local direction, then the curve represents the direction of the force throughout its course.

In modern terminology, such a curve is a *field line*. I retain the older conception because it does more than label a direction: it furnishes a geometrical object through which the action in space may be followed continuously. The direction is not merely assigned at isolated points and then forgotten; it is woven into a connected family of curves filling space and rendering the distributed action visible to the mind.

This is already a major gain. A law that would otherwise be presented as a collection of independent force vectors at distinct positions is replaced by a continuous geometrical system. The force can now be traced, compared, and grouped by visible structure. A student is no longer required to infer the whole arrangement from detached formulas alone.

At this stage the line expresses only direction. Yet even this is significant, for it transfers the phenomena from the language of isolated action between bodies to the language of continuous order in space. The later mathematics will only refine what the line already suggests.

Direction alone is insufficient. I must also represent intensity. I therefore replace each line by a narrow tube carrying an incompressible imaginary fluid. The local speed of the fluid, together with the cross-sectional area of the tube, provides a measure of the strength of the action. If the section narrows, the speed increases; if it widens, the speed decreases. In this way both direction and intensity are represented geometrically.

The passage from line to tube is essential. So long as I possess only a curve, I know where the action points, but not how much of it there is. Once a tube is introduced, sectional magnitude becomes available, and with it a quantitative representation of the action. The amount carried through a section and the speed with which it is conveyed are now combined in a single spatial construction.

For electric and magnetic actions of the class now under consideration, the tubes may be taken to fill space without interstices. The action is then represented by the steady motion of an incompressible medium through all space. In modern language, one may speak here of *field direction*, *field magnitude*, and *flux* through a section. I retain the medium-based representation because it is the vehicle by which these relations are first made intelligible and calculable.

This representation is superior, for the present purpose, to beginning at once with forces acting between distant particles. Action at a distance states the law, but it does not readily present the intermediate spatial organization by which the law is made tractable. The tube-and-medium picture, by contrast, places before the mind a connected system in which local geometry, local intensity, and transmission through space all appear at once.

It is by this means that I prepare the way for the more difficult parts of the inquiry. Once the student is accustomed to thinking of the action as distributed through a medium, the later relations of pressure, continuity, resistance, and induction arise naturally as properties of a single connected scheme rather than as a mere heap of formulas.

# Part 02: A Mechanical Fluid Analogy for Lines of Force

I now formalize the imaginary fluid. It is not introduced as an actual substance, but as a mathematical medium possessing only the properties required for the argument. The essential property is incompressibility: a portion of fluid that occupies a given volume at one instant occupies an equal volume at any later instant. Quantity of fluid is therefore measured by volume.

This idealization is deliberate. By suppressing everything except motion and incompressibility, I obtain a medium in which spatial transmission can be reasoned about clearly without burdening the analysis with irrelevant mechanical detail. The medium has no claim to independent physical reality beyond what is required to keep the geometric and quantitative relations before the mind.

In particular, I do not ask what the fluid is made of, whether it has ordinary weight, whether it is visible, or whether it resembles any known material. None of these questions assists the present argument. The sole purpose of the medium is to carry a conserved quantity through space under laws that can be represented geometrically and calculated analytically.

At each point of the space occupied by the fluid there is a definite direction of motion. I may therefore draw a curve whose tangent at every point agrees with the local velocity. In later language, these curves are close to what would be called *streamlines*, and in steady motion they are also the paths of individual fluid particles. Since the cases required here are steady, the geometrical curve and the physical path coincide.

This coincidence is of practical importance. It means that the same line serves at once as a geometrical guide and as a physical trajectory within the analogy. The student may therefore reason about the form of the curve and about the passage of the fluid along it without the need to separate two different constructions.

The representation also enforces locality. The motion is not given by a global formula alone; it is specified point by point, with each element of the curve carrying the direction proper to its own position. In that respect it anticipates a later language of distributed action in space, though the original *lines of fluid motion* remain the primary description here.

If a closed curve cuts these lines of motion, the lines drawn from it generate a tubular surface. Because the fluid velocity is everywhere tangent to this surface, no fluid crosses its sides. The tube is therefore dynamically equivalent to an impermeable pipe. Since the fluid is incompressible, the flow rate through every cross-section of the same tube is constant.

This constancy of transmission is the key quantitative fact. Whatever enters the tube must emerge, and it must do so at the same rate, because there is neither compression within the tube nor leakage through its sides. The tube is therefore a natural carrier of numerical measure. It is not merely a convenient drawing; it is the elementary conduit by which the whole motion is parcelled out.

I may then define a unit tube as one through which unit volume passes per unit time. Once space is decomposed into such tubes, the motion is completely specified: the direction is that of the tube, and the speed is the reciprocal of its cross-sectional area. Narrowing of the tube implies increase of speed; widening implies decrease. Geometry and magnitude thus become inseparable.

To construct the whole motion, I choose a surface cutting all lines of motion and divide it into quadrilateral patches, each transmitting unit volume per unit time. Extending the bounding curves of these patches along the flow produces a full lattice of unit tubes. One may compare this, cautiously, to a later *flux partition* of space into elementary *streamtubes*, but the unit-tube construction remains the primary description.

By this construction the medium is decomposed into a countable architecture of equal-conveyance channels. Every point of the occupied space lies within some tube, and every tube carries the same assigned quantity in the same assigned time. The complexity of the motion is therefore not denied, but organized.

The representation is powerful because it turns local motion into geometry. Once the unit tubes are known, both direction and magnitude follow immediately. Moreover, by refining the assumed unit, the construction may be made as delicate as the argument requires, so that finite figures may stand in for limiting processes without loss of generality.

A tube may close on itself, giving *circulation*, or it may begin and end at different places. If it begins or ends within the region under consideration, I represent that event by a *source* or a *sink*. A source creates unit fluid per unit time; a sink removes it. Since the medium is imaginary, there is no contradiction in admitting creation and annihilation at designated points. These are bookkeeping devices by which distributed production and absorption are represented.

The introduction of *sources* and *sinks* is not a weakness of the analogy but one of its chief strengths. It allows local production and destruction to be represented without ambiguity, and thereby gives a direct geometrical account of how flux through a closed surface is connected with what lies inside it. The medium, though imaginary, now supports a precise local accounting.

The total outward *flux* through a closed surface is therefore equal to the net *source strength* enclosed. In modern structural notation this bookkeeping has the form

$$
\int_{\partial V}\mathbf{v}\cdot d\mathbf{S}=S_{\mathrm{inside}},
$$

where $\mathbf{v}$ denotes the fluid velocity field and $S_{\mathrm{inside}}$ is the net source strength enclosed by the closed surface. This is a bridge to later divergence language, not a replacement for the source-and-sink construction. I retain the language of sources because it keeps before the mind the concrete image of generation and absorption from which the counting argument proceeds.

# Part 03: Sources, Potential, and Media in the Fluid Analogy

The intersections of the *unit tubes* with successive surfaces of equal pressure divide the flow into elementary portions which I call *unit cells*. In each such cell, unit volume of fluid passes in unit time from pressure $p$ to pressure $p-1$, and therefore overcomes one unit of resistance. This gives a direct energetic meaning to the geometrical construction. The motion is not merely drawn; it is measured cell by cell through the work required to sustain it. The equal-pressure surfaces and the *unit tubes* together provide not only kinematics and transmission, but a distributed reckoning of expenditure.

Thus every cell becomes an elementary act of the theory: one unit conveyed, one unit of pressure descended, one unit of work consumed. The whole medium may therefore be analyzed by summing identical operations repeated through space.

If the *pressure* is constant on a closed surface and there are no enclosed *sources* or *sinks*, then no motion can occur within that surface. The fluid has nowhere to descend in pressure, nowhere to be created, and nowhere to be destroyed. The interior pressure must therefore be uniform.

The proof is as physical as it is mathematical. A tube wholly contained within the surface cannot carry circulation, because the fluid must always descend from greater pressure to less, and no return to the same value is admissible in such a cycle. A tube terminating on the boundary cannot sustain motion either, because both ends would lie on the same pressure level. Since no tube can begin or end in the interior, none can exist there at all. The pressure must therefore be the same everywhere within.

From this it follows that once the boundary pressure and the enclosed *source* distribution are fixed, there can be only one admissible pressure field. If two existed, their difference would define a third system with zero boundary pressure and no interior sources, which is impossible except for the trivial zero solution. This is the uniqueness theorem for the present theory, and it guarantees that every legitimate construction, once found, is the construction.

# Part 04: Variable Resistance and Anisotropic Conduction

In the external medium the imagined auxiliary *sources* must neutralize one another in the aggregate, so that the outer region becomes an infinite medium free from net internal production. The *pressure* at infinity is then zero, while the pressure on the boundary of the inner region is constant. If that boundary pressure were positive everywhere, the fluid would have to flow outward from every point of the surface; if it were negative everywhere, the fluid would have to flow inward at every point. But neither case is admissible, because whatever quantity enters the boundary at one place must be matched by an equal quantity leaving it elsewhere.

The boundary pressure in the auxiliary difference-system must therefore be zero. Once that is established, the interior also has zero pressure throughout, because in the zero-resistance case the interior cannot sustain a pressure variation independently of the boundary value. The supposed difference between two admissible constructions disappears, and the auxiliary source arrangement is shown to be unique.

This completes the argument promised in the preceding part. A region of vanishing resistance behaves as a region of uniform *potential*, but that uniformity is not asserted arbitrarily; it is enforced by the flux balance and by the impossibility of maintaining a directional motion under a single constant boundary level.

The zero-resistance interior limit remains primary here and should not be replaced by a broader conductor doctrine.

If the interior resistance is infinite, the fluid can neither penetrate nor move within that region. The bounding surface therefore behaves as an *impermeable boundary*, and the *lines of motion* outside it must run tangentially along the surface rather than cross it. The internal region is dynamically disconnected from the outer one.

This converts the whole problem into an external boundary-value problem. If I can assign auxiliary *sources* inside the excluded region so that the computed outer motion satisfies the condition of zero normal velocity at the surface, then the resulting outer *pressure* system is the correct one. The interior need not be solved at all, because the absence of flux across the boundary makes it irrelevant to the external motion.

The significance of the argument is methodological. A region of infinite resistance is treated not by inventing a new principle, but by taking the same medium theory to its opposite limiting case. Zero resistance gave a constant-*potential* interior; infinite resistance gives an excluded region bounded by tangential flow.

The historical impermeable-boundary wording remains primary in this limiting transition.

# Part 05: General Propositions and the Electrostatic Analogy

In the most general medium, once the distribution of *pressure* is known, the equal-pressure surfaces, the *unit tubes*, and the *sources* and *sinks* may still be constructed as before. Each cell through which one unit of fluid passes while the pressure falls from $p$ to $p-1$ still accounts for one unit of *work*. The earlier cell bookkeeping therefore survives the move from uniform resistance to the most general direction-dependent case.

The number of cells belonging to a single tube is counted by the pressure difference between its ends. If a tube runs from pressure $p$ to pressure $p'$, then it contains $p-p'$ cells. From that, I pass to the total count for an entire system: a *source* of strength `S` at pressure $p$ contributes `Sp`, while a *sink* contributes the same quantity with negative sign. The total *work* is therefore written as the sum $W = \Sigma(Sp)$ when sinks are reckoned as negative sources.

This work-counting argument remains in source-and-cell language rather than in a later energy formalism.

### Modernized Section Heading: Applying Field-Line Geometry

I now turn from the fluid construction itself to its intended physical use. I propose to adapt the earlier picture to statical electricity, permanent magnetism, induced magnetism, and steady galvanic currents. In this first step, the electrostatic case is made explicit.

The move is to replace each particle of positive electricity by a fluid *source* whose velocity varies as $1/r^2$, exactly as the electrical attraction varies with distance. In modern bridge notation, the source construction gives the same radial dependence as

$$
|\mathbf{v}(r)|\propto \frac{1}{r^{2}}.
$$

When all such sources are combined, the resultant fluid velocity is proportional to the resultant electrical attraction. The rate of decrease of *pressure* in a chosen direction therefore tracks the resolved electrical action in that direction.

This is still analogy, not identity. The historical *lines of force* picture must stay visible rather than being flattened immediately into a later *electric field* formalism.

I now turn to dielectrics. The effect of electrical induction depends not only on the electrified bodies themselves, but also on the nature of the interposed medium. With Faraday, I shall describe this by saying that one substance has greater *inductive capacity* than another, or conducts the lines of inductive action more freely. In the fluid analogy, this corresponds to a medium whose resistance is smaller.

By the earlier two-medium construction, there is in this case an apparent distribution of electricity on the surface of the dielectric: negative where the lines enter and positive where they emerge. In the fluid case these are merely computational surface sources and sinks, not real ones. The dielectric case is treated with the same caution. The surface effect may represent an apparent electrical action rather than a literal charge distribution asserted as independently real.

If the dielectric is nearly perfect in conduction for the small quantities under discussion, it reduces to the earlier conductor case. Its surface then becomes one of equal *potential*, and the resultant attraction near the surface is perpendicular to it.

# Part 07: Current Electricity, Electromotive Force, and Closed Currents

The theory of fluid motion finds its most direct application in the laws of constant electric currents. The received picture is that a current of fluid moves uniformly through conducting circuits, meeting resistance that must be overcome by an electromotive action somewhere in the circuit. That resistance implies different values of *electrical tension* at different points.

This gives the key bridge back to the earlier electrostatic work: this *electrical tension* is physically identical with the *potential* of statical electricity. The fluid analogy therefore connects the current problem with the statical one through the same scalar quantity, even though the moving case adds steady circulation and circuit-driving agency.

The block closes by stressing that differences among materials are differences of degree in finite resistance. On that reading, dielectric induction already proceeds by conduction, but with quantities so small as to be insensible because the dielectric resistance is so great.

# Part 08: Induction, the Electro-Tonic State, and Current Quantity

I now restate Faraday's induction law in the language of *lines of force*. A conductor moving transversely across those lines acquires an *electromotive force* tending to drive a current if the circuit is closed, or to produce tension if it is open. For a closed moving conductor, no net current appears when the number of magnetic lines threading the circuit stays unchanged. The effective agency is therefore the change in the number of lines cut during the motion.

The directional rule is sharper. If more lines pass through the circuit after the motion than before, the induced current runs opposite to the current that would have created those additional lines. If the number of lines is diminished, the induced current tends to restore them. The same electromotive effect follows whether the increase or decrease in linked magnetic action comes from moving the conductor, moving other conductors or magnets, changing the strength of neighboring currents, or magnetizing and demagnetizing nearby bodies.

From that invariance I infer, cautiously, that the effect points to a changing state of the circuit measured by the number of lines through it. This inference remains historical-first, and the state is not yet identified with any later formal object.

I quote Faraday's own description of the *electro-tonic state*: while the wire is under volta-electric or magno-electric induction, it is in a peculiar condition that resists the formation of a current, yet when that influence is removed it can itself originate one. Faraday presents this as a newly named electrical condition of matter, not as a finished mechanism.

I do not claim that this state is already established physical truth. I say only that a conjecture from a thinker as experimentally reliable as Faraday may contain more truth than many later empirical regularities, and that it may still be valuable as a mathematical idea even if not yet forced on us as ontology. This is a narrow endorsement: productive, not conclusive.

The methodological consequence is clear. Because the electro-tonic state has not yet appeared to me in an embodied system of lines or surfaces, I shall use symbols freely in what follows and hope that the theory of elastic solids or viscous fluids may eventually furnish a mechanical conception. ### Modernized Section Heading: Part II: Faraday's Electro-Tonic State

I now take up Faraday's *electro-tonic state*. I first restate the observational basis: when a conductor lies near a current or magnet, or when those neighboring agents move or change strength, electric tension or a continuous current can appear; if the surrounding circumstances remain fixed, no observed effect follows. Even so, the conductor must be in different states under those different surrounding conditions, because removing the neighboring current or magnet can itself generate a current.

That latent-state argument is what led Faraday to name the *electro-tonic state*. I preserve Faraday's later hesitation, but I also insist that the established induction laws are stated with enough clarity to serve as premises for mathematical investigation. The mathematician's role is not to replace experiment but to arrange experimentally grounded ideas so that additional testable laws can be extracted.

The program of the following investigation is therefore this: assume Faraday's laws as true, pursue the broader consequences of his speculations, and see whether mathematical connections reveal new physical connections. That is the governing rule for the symbolic development that follows.

# Part 10: Electromagnetism: Ampere's Laws and Shell Currents

### Modernized Section Heading: Electromagnetism

I now enter the subject of *electro-magnetism*. Ampere's laws of the attractions and repulsions of *electric currents* supply the experimental starting point.

First, equal and opposite currents generate equal and opposite forces. Secondly, a crooked current is equivalent to a straight one when the two currents nearly coincide throughout their whole length. Thirdly, equal currents traversing similar and similarly situated closed curves act with equal forces, whatever the linear dimensions of those circuits may be. Fourthly, a *closed current* exerts no force tending to turn a circular conductor about its centre.

These laws are kept here as stated empirical restrictions. They do not yet authorize the following calculation of magnetizing force around a current, and they do not by themselves authorize a force law for isolated current elements.

# Part 18: Summary: Six Laws of the Electro-Tonic State

The electro-tonic state may now be summarized. At any point of space it may be conceived as a quantity with magnitude and direction. A portion of space may therefore be represented by any mechanical system that assigns, at every point, some directed quantity such as a velocity, displacement, or force corresponding in direction and magnitude to the supposed electro-tonic state.

This representation is not a physical theory. It is an artificial notation. In analysis, I use the three components of the electro-tonic state and call them electro-tonic functions.

For a closed curve, I take the resolved part of the electro-tonic intensity at each point of the curve and integrate it around the curve. A compact structural bridge is

$$
\oint_C \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{0}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell},
$$

but this notation only abbreviates the historical "entire electro-tonic intensity round the curve."

Proposition I states that if a closed curve is drawn on a surface and the enclosed surface is divided into small areas, the entire intensity around the closed curve equals the sum of the intensities around the small areas, all estimated in the same direction. The reason is cancellation: each interior boundary is traversed twice in opposite directions, so its two contributions destroy one another. Only the exterior boundary remains.

Law I applies this to magnetic induction. The entire electro-tonic intensity around the boundary of an element of surface measures the quantity of magnetic induction passing through that surface, or the number of lines of magnetic force through it. By Proposition I, the same statement extends from elementary surfaces to finite surfaces; any two surfaces with the same boundary have the same quantity of magnetic induction through them.

Law II states that magnetic intensity at a point is connected with the quantity of magnetic induction by linear equations called equations of conduction. This keeps the historical quantity/intensity language and does not impose a later constitutive notation.

# Part 19: Weber's Electrodynamic Theory and the Value of a Second Representation

There is, however, another theory of electrodynamics which professes to be physical. It is elegant, mathematical, and quite different from the method used here, so its assumptions must be stated.

First, two particles of electricity in motion do not repel one another with the same force as when at rest. The force is altered by a quantity depending on their relative motion, so that the repulsion at distance $r$ is written

$$
{\frac{ee'}{r^{2}}}
\left(1+a{\overline{\frac{dr}{dt}}}{\Bigg\vert}^{2}
+br{\frac{d^{2}r}{dt^{2}}}\right).
$$

This is Weber's historical formula as printed here. No modern normalization of constants, signs, or derivative notation is being made.

Second, when electricity moves in a conductor, the velocity of the positive fluid relative to the matter of the conductor is equal and opposite to that of the negative fluid. Third, the total action of one conducting element on another is the resultant of the mutual actions of the electric masses of both kinds contained in each element.

<details>
<summary>Original text: lines 2086-2097</summary>

> (1) That two particles of electricity when in motion do not repel each

>
> ${\frac {ee'}{r^{2}}}\left(1+a{\overline {\frac {dr}{dt}}}{\Bigg \vert }^{2}+br{\frac {d^{2}r}{dt^{2}}}\right).$

The answer is that it is useful to have two ways of looking at the subject, and to admit that there are two ways. There is no present right to claim that the action of electricity is understood. A temporary theory is most useful when it guides experiment without obstructing the true theory when it appears.

There are also objections to making ultimate forces in nature depend on the velocity of the bodies between which they act. Let an electric current pass through a mass of metal of any form. The distribution of the currents within the metal is determined by the laws of conduction.

Now let a constant electric current pass through another conductor near the first. If the two currents are in the same direction, the material conductors are attracted toward one another and would approach if they were not held in place. But although the material conductors are attracted, the currents, being free to choose their course within the metal, do not alter their original distribution or incline toward each other.

Since no change takes place in the system, there are no electromotive forces to modify the original distribution of currents. In this case electromagnetic forces act on the material conductor without any electromotive forces tending to modify the current which it carries.

As the opposite example, take a linear conductor which does not form a closed circuit, and let it traverse the lines of magnetic force, either by its own motion or by changes in the magnetic field. An electromotive force acts along the conductor. Since there is no circuit, this force cannot produce a current; it produces electric tension at the extremities instead.

There is no electromagnetic attraction on the material conductor, because that attraction depends on the existence of a current within it, and the open circuit prevents such a current. Thus there is an electromotive force acting on the electricity in the conductor, but no attraction on its material particles.

I add the remaining notes here. The cited relation for the inductive capacity of the sphere is written, in this notation, as

$$

=

$$

In the more general magnetic-induction case referred to earlier, a constant term depending on $T$ would make a positive amount of work appear in a complete revolution in the negative direction round the axis of $T$. Since no inexhaustible source of work exists in nature, $T=0$ must be admitted for magnetic induction in all substances. This argument does not hold for electric conduction, or for a body through which heat or electricity is passing, because such states are maintained by continual expenditure of work.

Finally, the expression for $p_{2}$ indicates a variable electric tension in the shell, so currents might be collected by wires touching it at the equator and poles.
