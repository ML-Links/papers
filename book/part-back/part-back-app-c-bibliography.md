## C.1 Primary Historical Sources

### Maxwell's Original Works

1. **Maxwell, J. C. (1858).** *On Faraday's Lines of Force*. Transactions of the Cambridge Philosophical Society, Vol. X, Part I.
   - **Annotation:** The primary text of this exegesis. Maxwell's first attempt to mathematize Faraday's qualitative lines of force using the fluid analogy.
   - **Full Text:** Available via Wikisource or Princeton's digital archives.

2. **Maxwell, J. C. (1873).** *A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism*. Oxford: Clarendon Press. (Vol. I & II).
   - **Annotation:** The mature, pedagogical synthesis of Maxwell's electromagnetic theory. Used as a supplementary text to clarify and refine the arguments of Paper 1.
   - **Full Text:** Available via Wikisource.

3. **Maxwell, J. C. (1890).** *The Scientific Papers of James Clerk Maxwell*. W. D. Niven (Ed.). Cambridge University Press.
   - **Annotation:** The authoritative collection of Maxwell's scientific papers, including *On Faraday's Lines of Force* and *On Physical Lines of Force*.

### Heaviside's Reformulation

4. **Heaviside, O. (1893).** *Electromagnetic Theory*. London: The Electrician Printing and Publishing Company. (Vol. I, II, III).
   - **Annotation:** Heaviside's reformulation of Maxwell's component equations into the four modern vector equations. Discards the fluid analogy entirely, while criticizing the physical reality of the vector potential \(\mathbf{A}\) and advocating for the primacy of the physical fields \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{H}\).
   - **Full Text:** Available via Internet Archive.

## C.2 Secondary Historical and Philosophical Commentaries

5. **Bork, A. M. (1964).** The Fourth Dimension in Nineteenth-Century Physics. *Isis*, 55(3), 326–338. https://www.jstor.org/stable/228574
   - **Annotation:** Explores the conceptual development of higher-dimensional thinking in 19th-century physics, providing context for the mathematical abstractions that influenced Maxwell's field theory and its reception.

6. **Bork, A. M. (1966).** Physics Just Before Einstein. *Science*, New Series, 152(3722), 597–603. https://www.jstor.org/stable/1718979
   - **Annotation:** Historical analysis of the state of physics in 1855, including the resistance to field concepts and the terminological evolution of the "field." Used extensively in Chapter 2. Documents the lack of efficient mathematical notation as a key factor delaying the development of electromagnetic theory.

7. **Bork, A. M. (1967).** Maxwell and the Vector Potential. *Isis*, 58(2), 210–222. https://www.jstor.org/stable/228226
   - **Annotation:** A detailed historical study of Maxwell's use of the vector potential, tracing its development from the "electro-tonic state" in Paper 1 to its mature formulation. Essential for understanding Part II of the exegesis and Maxwell's view of \(\mathbf{A}\) as a fundamental physical quantity.

8. **Bork, A. M. (2024).** Maxwell and the Development of Electromagnetic Theory. *American Journal of Physics*, 92, 498–510. https://doi.org/10.1119/5.0183355
   - **Annotation:** A comprehensive, nearly 50-year-old manuscript published posthumously, written in 1976 as an introduction to a collection of Maxwell's papers. Provides an authoritative overview of Maxwell's intellectual journey and serves as a guide for readers approaching the original texts.

9. **Darrigol, O. (2000).** *Electrodynamics from Ampère to Einstein*. Oxford University Press.
   - **Annotation:** A comprehensive history of electrodynamics, including detailed analysis of Maxwell's contributions and the reception of his work.

10. **Harman, P. M. (1982).** *Energy, Force, and Matter: The Conceptual Development of Nineteenth-Century Physics*. Cambridge University Press.
    - **Annotation:** Provides the broader scientific context for Maxwell's work, including the transition from action-at-a-distance to field theories.

11. **Williams, L. P. (1966).** *The Origins of Field Theory*. Random House.
    - **Annotation:** Provides the intellectual context for Maxwell's work, tracing the development of field concepts from Faraday to Maxwell.

## C.3 Contemporary Textbooks

### Vector Calculus and Mathematical Methods

12. **Arfken, G. B., & Weber, H. J. (2012).** *Mathematical Methods for Physicists*. Elsevier.
    - **Annotation:** Comprehensive reference for mathematical methods, including vector calculus, potential theory, and special functions.

13. **Schey, H. M. (2005).** *Div, Grad, Curl, and All That: An Informal Text on Vector Calculus*. W. W. Norton & Company.
    - **Annotation:** Used for Appendix A (Prerequisite Foundations) and as a reference for vector calculus operations encountered in Paper 1.

14. **Oregon State University Physics Department.** *The Geometry of Vector Calculus*. https://books.physics.oregonstate.edu/GVC/GVC.html
    - **Annotation:** An open-access online textbook that emphasizes the geometric intuition behind vector calculus. Written in PreTeXt and incorporates interactive applets in GeoGebra and SageMath. Useful for developing the visual understanding required for Maxwell's field geometry.

15. **Oregon State University Physics Department.** *Online Vector Calculus Books*. https://books.physics.oregonstate.edu/
    - **Annotation:** A collection of open-access vector calculus textbooks written in PreTeXt with interactive applets. These resources provide an accessible, visually oriented approach to the mathematical foundations required for this exegesis.

### Electromagnetism

16. **Feynman, R. P., Leighton, R. B., & Sands, M. (1964).** *The Feynman Lectures on Physics, Vol. II*. Addison-Wesley.
    - **Annotation:** Used for conceptual understanding of field theory and the physical interpretation of Maxwell's equations.

17. **Fleisch, D. (2008).** *A Student's Guide to Maxwell's Equations*. Cambridge University Press. https://www.cambridge.org/highereducation/books/a-students-guide-to-maxwells-equations/CB8D80B70E695AB8553D1F1117085AE3#overview
    - **Annotation:** A pedagogical guide that explains each of Maxwell's four equations in detail, with a focus on the physical meaning of divergence and curl. Useful for translating Maxwell's component equations into modern vector form.

18. **Griffiths, D. J. (2017).** *Introduction to Electrodynamics*. Cambridge University Press.
    - **Annotation:** Used for modern physical interpretation of Maxwell's equations and as a reference for the modern vector formulation.

19. **Jackson, J. D. (1999).** *Classical Electrodynamics*. John Wiley & Sons.
    - **Annotation:** Advanced reference for classical electrodynamics, including the Maxwell stress tensor and electromagnetic wave propagation.

### Classical Mechanics (for Future Papers)

20. **Taylor, J. R. (2005).** *Classical Mechanics*. University Science Books.
    - **Annotation:** Used for Lagrangian mechanics, which will be crucial for Papers 2 and 3.
