## C.1 Primary Historical Sources

### Maxwell's Original Works

1. **Maxwell, J. C. (1858).** *On Faraday's Lines of Force*. Transactions of the Cambridge Philosophical Society, Vol. X, Part I.
   - **Annotation:** The primary text of this exegesis. Maxwell's first attempt to mathematize Faraday's qualitative lines of force using the fluid analogy.
   - **Full Text:** Available via Wikisource or Princeton's digital archives.

2. **Maxwell, J. C. (1873).** *A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism*. Oxford: Clarendon Press. (Vol. I & II).
   - **Annotation:** The mature, pedagogical synthesis of Maxwell's electromagnetic theory. Used as a supplementary text to clarify and refine the arguments of Paper 1.
   - **Full Text:** Available via Wikisource.

3. **Maxwell, J. C. (1890).** *The Scientific Papers of James Clerk Maxwell*. W. D. Niven (Ed.). Cambridge University Press.
   - **Annotation:** The authoritative collection of Maxwell's scientific papers, including *On Faraday's Lines of Force* and *On Physical Lines of Force*.

### Heaviside's Reformulation

4. **Heaviside, O. (1893).** *Electromagnetic Theory*. London: The Electrician Printing and Publishing Company. (Vol. I, II, III).
   - **Annotation:** Heaviside's reformulation of Maxwell's component equations into the four modern vector equations. Discards the fluid analogy entirely, while criticizing the physical reality of the vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ and advocating for the primacy of the physical fields $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$.
   - **Full Text:** Available via Internet Archive.

## C.2 Secondary Historical and Philosophical Commentaries

5. **Bork, A. M. (1966).** Physics just before Einstein. *Science*, 152(3722), 597–603.
   - **Annotation:** Historical analysis of the state of physics in 1855, including the resistance to field concepts and the terminological evolution of the "field." Used extensively in Chapter 2.

6. **Williams, L. P. (1966).** *The Origins of Field Theory*. Random House.
   - **Annotation:** Provides the intellectual context for Maxwell's work, tracing the development of field concepts from Faraday to Maxwell.

7. **Darrigol, O. (2000).** *Electrodynamics from Ampère to Einstein*. Oxford University Press.
   - **Annotation:** A comprehensive history of electrodynamics, including detailed analysis of Maxwell's contributions and the reception of his work.

8. **Harman, P. M. (1982).** *Energy, Force, and Matter: The Conceptual Development of Nineteenth-Century Physics*. Cambridge University Press.
   - **Annotation:** Provides the broader scientific context for Maxwell's work, including the transition from action-at-a-distance to field theories.

## C.3 Contemporary Textbooks

### Vector Calculus and Mathematical Methods

9. **Schey, H. M. (2005).** *Div, Grad, Curl, and All That: An Informal Text on Vector Calculus*. W. W. Norton & Company.
   - **Annotation:** Used for Appendix A (Prerequisite Foundations) and as a reference for vector calculus operations encountered in Paper 1.

10. **Arfken, G. B., & Weber, H. J. (2012).** *Mathematical Methods for Physicists*. Elsevier.
    - **Annotation:** Comprehensive reference for mathematical methods, including vector calculus, potential theory, and special functions.

### Electromagnetism

11. **Griffiths, D. J. (2017).** *Introduction to Electrodynamics*. Cambridge University Press.
    - **Annotation:** Used for modern physical interpretation of Maxwell's equations and as a reference for the modern vector formulation.

12. **Jackson, J. D. (1999).** *Classical Electrodynamics*. John Wiley & Sons.
    - **Annotation:** Advanced reference for classical electrodynamics, including the Maxwell stress tensor and electromagnetic wave propagation.

13. **Feynman, R. P., Leighton, R. B., & Sands, M. (1964).** *The Feynman Lectures on Physics, Vol. II*. Addison-Wesley.
    - **Annotation:** Used for conceptual understanding of field theory and the physical interpretation of Maxwell's equations.

### Classical Mechanics (for Future Papers)

14. **Taylor, J. R. (2005).** *Classical Mechanics*. University Science Books.
    - **Annotation:** Used for Lagrangian mechanics, which will be crucial for Papers 2 and 3.
